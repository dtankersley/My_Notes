## Introduction
- The Complete [[Book of Enoch]], Standard English Version, is a manuscript translated from the original Ethiopic manuscript and logically organized by Dr. [[Jay Winter]], with the copyright held by Winter Publications since 2015.
- The book is made available to the public domain with the condition that it remains distributed free of charge, and it covers various topics including theology, ancient history, angelology, demonology, and biblical scriptures.
- The manuscript is divided into several books, including Book 1 - Watchers, which contains chapters on the blessing of [[Enoch]], the creation, the fallen angels, and the intercession of angels, among others.
- The subsequent books, including Book 2 - Parables, Book 3 - Book of [[Noah]], and Book 4 - Kingdom of Heaven, contain chapters that explore themes such as the parables, the birth and calling of Noah, the judgement of angels, and the luminaries of heaven.
- Book 5 - Epistle of Enoch contains chapters that provide guidance, wisdom, and revelations from Enoch, and the manuscript also includes extras such as a Dead Sea Scroll Chart, the Book of the Giants, and references to Enoch in other manuscripts.
- The Book of Enoch, also known as 1 Enoch or Ethiopian Enoch, was once widely accepted by Jews and Christians, but later fell into disfavor due to its controversial statements on the nature and deeds of the fallen angels, and it has been made available to the public domain through Dr. Jay Winter's translation and publication.

## The Book of Enoch
- The Library of Congress Cataloging-in-Publication Data for the book includes the author's name, Dr. [[Jay Winter]], and the ISBN number, 9781370207848, which categorizes the book under theology, ancient history, angelology, demonology, and biblical scriptures.
- The Enochian writings, along with other excluded or lost texts from the Bible, such as the Book of Tobit and Esdras, were recognized by early church fathers as "Apocryphal" writings, a term derived from the Greek word meaning "hidden" or "secret".
- Initially, the term "apocrypha" may have been complimentary, implying that the contents of these sacred books were too exalted to be made available to the general public, as hinted in Daniel 12:9-10, where words are shut up until the end of time and only the wise shall understand.
- The book of 4 Ezra 14:44ff mentions 94 books, of which 24 were to be published and 70 were to be delivered only to the wise among the people, further emphasizing the idea of hidden or secret knowledge.
- Over time, the term "apocrypha" took on a pejorative connotation, as the orthodoxy of these hidden books was often questionable, leading [[Origen]] to distinguish between books to be read in public worship and apocryphal books in his commentary on Matthew 10:18.
- The preservation of these secret books within esoteric circles of divinely-knit believers led to criticism from some Church Fathers, who deemed them heretical and forbidden to be read, applying the term "apocryphal" to works they considered to be outside the realm of understanding.
- As a result, the term "apocrypha" became associated with heretical works, rather than the original meaning of hidden or secret knowledge, highlighting the complex and evolving nature of the term's connotation throughout history.
- The Apocrypha refers to 15 works, mostly of Jewish origin, that are found in the Septuagint, but were not accepted into the Jewish canon formed in the 2nd century AD, and as a result, were not considered equal to other Scriptures by Protestant Reformers.
- The [[Catholic Church]], on the other hand, considers 12 of these 15 works to be canonical Scripture, referring to them as the Deuterocanonical Books, with the exception of 1-2 Esdras and the Prayer of Manasseh, which are not accepted by Catholics.
- The [[Book of Enoch]], which deals with the nature and deeds of fallen angels, was condemned by Church fathers, such as Filastrius, who labeled it as heresy, and was also rejected by rabbis, including Rabbi Simeon ben Jochai, who pronounced a curse upon those who believed in it.
- As a result of this condemnation, the Book of [[Enoch]] was denounced, banned, and lost for over a thousand years, but it was rediscovered in the 18th century when Scottish explorer James Bruce found three Ethiopic copies of the book in Ethiopia, where it had been preserved by the Ethiopic church.
- The Book of Enoch was eventually translated into English by Dr. Richard Laurence, a Hebrew professor at Oxford, in 1821, which introduced the modern world to the previously forbidden mysteries of Enoch, and marked the book's return to circulation after centuries of being lost and forgotten.
- The Book of Enoch is believed to have been written in its present form during the second century B.C. and was widely popular for at least five hundred years, with the earliest Ethiopic text being translated from a Greek manuscript that was a copy of an earlier text written in a Semitic language, now thought to be Aramaic.
- The book's origins are shrouded in obscurity, but recent discoveries of copies among the [[Dead Sea Scrolls]] found at [[Qumran]] have proven that it existed before the time of [[Jesus | Jesus Christ]], despite initial beliefs that it was written after Christianity emerged due to its similarities to Christian terminology and teaching.
- Historians have largely agreed that the book does not contain the authentic words of the ancient biblical patriarch Enoch, who would have lived several thousand years earlier than the first known appearance of the book attributed to him, according to the chronologies in the Book of Genesis.
- Despite its unknown origins, the [[Book of Enoch]] was once accepted as authentic scripture by Christians, particularly the part about the fallen angels and their prophesied judgment, and many key concepts used by Jesus Christ seem directly connected to terms and ideas in the Book of Enoch.
- The book's influence on Jesus Christ is evident, with over a hundred phrases in the New Testament finding precedents in the Book of [[Enoch]], and it is hard to avoid the conclusion that Jesus not only studied the book but also respected it highly enough to adopt and elaborate on its specific descriptions of the coming kingdom and its theme of inevitable judgment descending upon "the wicked", a term often used in the Old Testament to describe the Watchers.
- The Book of Enoch contains a call to preserve the book unto future generations, as Enoch commands his son [[Methuselah]] to preserve the book so that it might not be lost to the ages, highlighting the importance of preserving the book's teachings and messages.
- The early Christians' acceptance of the Book of Enoch is evident in the mistranslation of Luke 9:35 in the King James Bible, which describes the transfiguration of Christ, where the original Greek verse refers to Jesus as "the Elect One", a term found fourteen times in the Book of Enoch.
- The Book of Jude also references the Book of Enoch, with Jude quoting Enoch's prophecies in verse 14 and making a direct reference to the Book of Enoch in verse 15, which suggests that the written prophecies of Enoch were available to Jude at that time, approximately 3400 years after Enoch.
- The discovery of fragments of ten Enoch manuscripts among the [[Dead Sea Scrolls]], as well as other Enochian literature, such as the [[Book of Jubilees]], indicates that the Book of Enoch was widely available and possibly used by the Essenes as a community prayer book or study text.
- The [[Book of Enoch]] was also used by writers of non-canonical texts, including the author of the Epistle of Barnabas, who quotes the Book of Enoch three times and refers to it as "the Scripture", denoting the inspired Word of God, and other apocryphal works, such as the Testaments of the Twelve Patriarchs and the Book of Jubilees, which reflect knowledge of the Enoch story of the Watchers.
- The references to the Book of [[Enoch]] in the Bible and other ancient texts provide significant scriptural authenticity to the Book of Enoch, particularly in its descriptions of the Elect One who should "sit upon the throne of glory" and "dwell in the midst of them", which is also mentioned in the voice from the cloud during the transfiguration of [[Jesus | Christ]].
- The early church fathers, including Justin Martyr, Athenagoras, Tatian, Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Tertullian, [[Origen]], Lactantius, Methodius of Philippi, Minucius Felix, Commodianus, and Ambrose of Milan, supported and approved of the Enochian writings, which suggests that these writings were highly regarded in the early Christian community.
- Athenagoras, in his work Legatio, written around 170 A.D., regarded Enoch as a true prophet and described the angels that "violated both their own nature and their office", which is a direct reference to the Enochian writings and their account of the fallen angels.
- The discovery of Aramaic Enochian texts among the Dead Sea Scrolls led to Catholic scholar J.T. Milik compiling a complete history of the Enochian writings, including translations of the Aramaic manuscripts, which was published in 1976 as a 400-page book titled "The Books of Enoch: Aramaic Fragments of [[Qumran]] Cave 4".
- J.T. Milik's work is considered a milestone in Enochian scholarship, and his opinions, based on years of in-depth research, are highly respected, which has helped to strengthen the credibility of the Enochian writings.
- The arguments against the [[Book of Enoch]], including its lack of historicity and late date, are fading away, and new evidence may soon silence the remaining complaints, potentially including the return of Enoch, who is believed to be still living in the Kingdom of Heaven and has not yet fulfilled the Scripture that states it is appointed unto man to die once.
- Spiritual scholars have attributed the fulfillment of the prophecies of [[Enoch]] to being revealed in [[Book of Revelation | Revelation]] 11, which includes the return of Enoch with a second reprimand of the world for three and a half years, suggesting that the Book of Enoch contains important prophetic information that is still relevant today.

## The Second Book of Enoch (The Parables)

- According to Enoch, the Lord of Spirits has given him a unique insight and the gift of eternal life, and has imparted three Parables to him, which he has recounted to those who dwell on the earth, starting with the first Parable.
- [[Enoch]] claims that the wisdom he has received from the Lord of Spirits is unparalleled and has never been given to anyone before, and he is now sharing this wisdom with others, as dictated by the Lord of Spirits.
- The righteous will appear and sinners will be judged for their sins, and the sinners will be driven from the face of the earth, with the dwelling of the sinners being a place of punishment and suffering, and it would have been good for them if they had not been born.

- The dwelling-places of the holy and the resting-places of the righteous will be revealed, with the Elect One of righteousness and of faith dwelling under the wings of the Lord of Spirits, and righteousness prevailing in His days, with the righteous and elect being without number before Him.

## The Story of Noah
- When [[Noah]] was born, he illuminated the entire house with his brightness, and his father [[Lamech (father of Noah) | Lamech]] was afraid of him, thinking he was not his son but rather an angel's child, due to his extraordinary appearance and abilities, with eyes like the rays of [[Sun | the sun]] and a countenance that was glorious.
- Lamech went to his father [[Methuselah]], telling him about the strange child and asking him to go to [[Enoch]], their ancestor, to learn the truth about the child, as Enoch dwelled amongst the angels and would know the truth.
- Methuselah then visited Enoch, who revealed to him that the child, Noah, was indeed Lamech's son, but he would be the one to bring comfort to the earth after a great destruction caused by the sin and unrighteousness of the people, and that Noah and his three children would be saved from the impending deluge.
- Enoch explained that some angels had transgressed the Lord's word, united with women, and had children, producing giants on the earth, which would lead to a great punishment and the cleansing of the earth from all impurity.
- Noah later saw the earth's destruction approaching and cried out to his grandfather Enoch, who told him that the Lord had determined the judgment of those who dwell on the earth due to their unrighteousness and the secrets they had learned from the angels, including sorcery and witchcraft.
- Enoch revealed to Noah that he was pure and guiltless of these secrets and that the Lord had destined him to be among the holy and to preserve him and his righteous seed, and that his name would be associated with great honors and kingship.
- The [[Lord of Spirits]] then spoke to [[Noah]], telling him that his lot had come up before Him, a lot of love and uprightness, and that He would preserve his seed and make the earth fruitful again, and that the angels who had shown unrighteousness would be imprisoned in a burning valley.
- The Lord also showed Noah the punishment of the angels who had led astray those on the earth, and how the waters in the valley would serve for the healing of the body but the punishment of the spirit for those who denied the Lord of Spirits.
- Michael, an angel, answered and said that the judgment of the angels was a testimony for the kings and the mighty who possess the earth, and that the waters of judgment would change and become a fire that burns forever, ministering to the healing of the body but also serving as a punishment for those who deny the Spirit of the Lord.
- Finally, [[Enoch]] gave Noah the teaching of all the secrets in the book of Parables, which had been given to him, and Michael and Raphael discussed the severity of the judgment of the secrets and the angels, with Michael saying that he would not take the part of those who had led the angels astray under the eye of the Lord.

## The Book of Giants
- The [[Dead Sea Scrolls]], found in the [[Qumran]] caves, contain a significant body of work that includes parts of [[The Book of Giants | the Book of Giants]], which is associated with Enoch, and this collection of texts represents a body of knowledge that has been largely suppressed since its inception.
- The Book of Giants is a text that has been published in multiple languages, including Syriac, Greek, Middle Persian, Sogdian, Uygur, and possibly Coptic, with the various versions being translated from one another, such as the Sogdian edition being derived from the Middle Persian version.
- The presence of certain names, like Sām and Narīmān, in the Arabic version of the Book of Giants indicates that it was translated from the Middle Persian version, and the text has been quoted frequently by Manichæan authors, with only one direct citation by a non-Manichæan writer.
- The surviving fragments of the Book of Giants, labeled as texts A-G, have been supplemented with two additional excerpts, including a significant one, labeled as text H, which likely derives from a Syriac epitome of the book, and these passages are primarily quotations from the Syriac edition.
- The majority of the passages referring to the Book of Giants, labeled as texts J-T, are based on Syriac writings and should be treated as quotations from the Syriac edition, rather than being considered as original texts, such as the Parthian text N, which was translated from a Syriac treatise that cited the Syriac text of the Book of Giants.
- The stories in the Book of the Giants were influenced by local traditions during their journey across Central Asia, resulting in the introduction of myths related to Iranian heroes, such as the translation of Ohya as Sām, which led to the concept of Sām's immortality according to text I.
- The country of Aryān-Vēžan, also known as Airyana Vaēǰah, mentioned in text G, is another example of an innovation that was incorporated into the Book of the Giants, similar to the possible connection between the "Kögmän mountains" in text B and "[[Mount Hermon]]".
- The progeny of the fallen angels was initially confined to thirty-six towns, as stated in text S, but this number was later changed to thirty-two in text G, possibly due to the introduction of Mount Sumeru, which is associated with the heaven of Indra and consists of thirty-two cities of devas.
- The translation of the Book of the Giants includes various notation systems, such as manuscript mapping keys, to indicate damaged or uncertain readings, suggested restorations of missing letters, and estimated numbers of missing letters, in order to provide a clear understanding of the text.
- The use of parentheses in the translation is employed for explanatory remarks, while bolded text highlights important and correlated fragments with the books of [[Enoch]], allowing readers to grasp the significance of the content.
- Despite the damaged state of the text from multiple manuscripts, the different versions of the book in various languages provide a general picture of what [[The Book of Giants | the Book of Giants]] is discussing, with logical estimates suggesting that the giants lived around 3500 BC and the manuscripts themselves are likely over 3000 years old, having been copied numerous times throughout the ages.
- The text appears to be a fragmented and translated section from the [[Book of Enoch | book of Enoch]], detailing various events and conversations between characters, including Sām, Shahmīzād, and Virōgdād, with discussions about giants, death, and the afterlife.
- Sām is quoted as saying "Blessed be... had he seen this, he would not have died," in response to a statement made by Shahmīzād, who claims that all of Māhawai is spoilt, and Sām's father, Virōgdād, is mentioned as a reference point for their conversation.
- The story also involves a giant named Hōbābīš, who robbed a mortal man named Ahr of his wife, Naxtag, leading to a series of violent events, including the giants killing each other and abducting their wives, as well as creatures killing each other.
- Sām is described as standing before [[Sun | the sun]] with one hand in the air and the other on the ground, and is said to have obtained something that he gave to his brother, who was imprisoned over Taxtag, and Taxtag was eventually thrown into the water.
- The text also mentions Taxtag seeing three signs in his sleep, including one portending woe and flight, and another annihilation, and Narīmān seeing a garden full of trees in rows, with two hundred trees coming out.
- [[Enoch]], the apostle, is quoted as giving a message to the demons and their children, stating that they will be bound for their sins and will see the destruction of their children, and will rule for a hundred and twenty years, with a list of various animals and objects, including a wild ass, ibex, ram, goat, gazelle, and oryx.
- The final section of the text appears to describe a scene involving a father and his nuptials, with a discussion about fighting and a nest, and a conversation between two brothers, Ohya and Ahya, who are instructed to take what their father has ordered them to and to prepare for battle, with the giants fighting them together, and a reference to the lion and the rainbow.

## The Testament of Solomon
- The [[Testament of Solomon]], which is attributed to [[Solomon]], the son of David, who was king in [[Jerusalem]], describes how Solomon mastered and controlled all spirits of the air, on the earth, and under the earth, and used them to build the Temple, and also tells of the authorities these spirits wield against men and how they are brought to naught by angels.
- The text also describes a specific incident where a demon named Ornias came among the workmen building the Temple, stole from a master workman's little boy, and sucked the thumb of his right hand every day, causing the child to grow thin, and King Solomon eventually called the boy and questioned him about the situation, showing his concern and love for the child.
- The king, Solomon, asks a child why he appears to be growing thinner every day, despite being given double wages and a double supply of food, and the child explains that a demon named Ornias comes to him every night and takes away half of his pay and food, and also sucks his thumb, causing his body to waste away.
- Solomon, upon hearing this, prays to the Lord Sabaoth in the Temple of God, asking for the power to capture the demon and gain authority over him, and through his prayer, he receives a little ring with a seal, a Pentalpha, also known as the seal of Solomon, from the archangel Michael.
- The ring is said to have the power to lock up all demons, male and female, and Solomon is instructed to wear the seal of God, which will allow him to build up Jerusalem with the help of the demons, and he is overjoyed and praises the God of heaven and earth upon receiving the ring.
- Solomon then gives the ring to the child and instructs him to throw it at the demon's chest when it comes to him, saying "In the name of God, King Solomon calls thee hither", and to come running to him afterwards, without fear of the demon's response.
- The child takes the ring and waits for the demon to arrive, and at the customary hour, the demon Ornias appears, coming like a burning fire to take the child's pay, setting the stage for the child to use the ring and summon the demon to [[Solomon]].
- The child, following King Solomon's instructions, threw a ring at the chest of the demon Ornias and said "King Solomon calls thee hither", after which the demon begged to be freed and offered the child gold in exchange, but the child refused and instead brought the demon to King Solomon.
- King Solomon questioned Ornias, who revealed that he was subject to the zodiacal sign of the Water-pourer, and that he strangled people who were consumed with desire for noble virgins, and also had the ability to metamorphose into different forms, including a comely female and a lion.
- Ornias was sealed and set to work at stone-cutting for the Temple, but he was fearful of the iron and begged to be freed, promising to bring all the demons to King Solomon, who then prayed to the archangel Uriel for assistance, and Uriel came down from the heavens and helped to subdue the demon.
- King Solomon then sent Ornias to bring the prince of all the demons, Beelzeboul, who was reluctant to come but eventually followed Ornias to King Solomon, and when Beelzeboul arrived, King Solomon questioned him and learned that he was the exarch of the demons and had the power to make apparitions manifest.
- Beelzeboul promised to bring all the unclean spirits to King Solomon, and King Solomon then asked to see the female demons, and Beelzeboul brought Onoskelis, who was a spirit made into a body and had the ability to strangle men and prevent them from knowing their true natures.
- Onoskelis revealed that she was born of a voice, or the echo of a man's ordure, and that she passed under the star of the full [[Moon | moon]], and that the angel Joel frustrated her, and King [[Solomon]] then commanded her to spin hemp for the ropes used in the building of the Temple.
- King Solomon then summoned another demon, Asmodeus, who was bound and questioned, and revealed that he was born of an angel's seed by a daughter of man, and that his business was to plot against the newly wedded and to estrange the hearts of virgin women.
- Asmodeus also revealed that he transported men into fits of madness and desire, causing them to leave their wives and commit sin, and that he was frustrated by the archangel Raphael, and that the liver and gall of a fish, specifically the glanos fish, could put him to flight.
- King Solomon then commanded Asmodeus to make clay for the construction of the Temple and to carry water in jars, and Asmodeus was forced to obey, despite his knowledge of the future and his reluctance to be subjected to King Solomon's will.
